RECIPE

1. 

Sift the flour with the cinnamon into a bowl. Sand the flour by dispersing the lard with your fingertips, add the sugar and the grated orange peel.

2. 

Mix the whole egg and continue kneading, pouring in the salt dissolved in the vinegar and the Marsala.

3. 

Roll out a third of the dough at a time, with the sheeter or rolling pin, until it reaches a thickness of 1.5 mm. Using a 10 cm diameter pastry cutter, cut out discs and stretch them slightly to create an oval.

4. 

Weld the external edges with the yolk, overlapping them along the midline of the torch. Cover the cannoli ready for frying with cling film, preventing them from drying on the surface, as this would compromise the development of bubbles during cooking. Immerse the cannoli in oil at 175°C, with the seal facing downwards.

5. 

Very crumbly bubbles will form on the surface of the cannoli, handle them delicately using a slotted spoon. In a few seconds they will be golden and ready to drain on frying paper. Wait for them to reach room temperature before removing the metal torch.

6. 

Fried scraps of pastry - or lozenges in the absence of the appropriate metal torches - can be used to create a "decomposed cannoli" alternating them with the ricotta cream. For the cream: sift the ricotta well drained from the whey, work it for a minute with the electric whisk, incorporating the icing sugar.

7. 

The cannoli wafers should be filled with the ricotta cream only when serving them, using a piping bag with a smooth 16 mm nozzle or a spatula. Decorate the ends according to your preferences: with icing sugar, candied cherries and oranges, dark chocolate, chopped pistachios, cinnamon or a mix.

The recipe for an irresistible dessert whose history comes from far away.
